# Statement on Public Comment Drafting

Over the last few years, I've written three comment letters and eight regulatory examination response letters. Of those documents, one of the comments was drafted entirely on GitHub after the migration of a few brainstorming pages, and one of the exam responses was drafted entirely on GitHub (the last one, which came out to about the length of a comment). All the comments are public, and five of the exam responses are public.[^2]

When the Commission started our first exam, I wanted to draft the initial reply on GitHub. However, as timing overlapped, I was just then finishing the last openly drafted comment. In the two days I had to respond to over 25 specific comply-or-die questions, I decided that I would slow-roll the open-sourcing of communications for the Syndicate and the staff.

I remember the little walk I took in the woods making this decision very clearly. Most people, and especially regulatory bureaucracies, are very used to private, proprietary communications. Those PDFs with the all-bold, all-caps warnings against reproduction and confidentiality. I find them most humorous on legal templates, which are literally just suggested starting points to achieve an end within the _public_ laws.

All that secrecy reminds me of early music printing, and to a lesser extent software. I remember getting a letter from my middle school affirming I was a student, just to access a discounted price on some creative music software growing up. Access to building materials, much less the emotional connection available through music, shouldn't be held back behind paywalls or secrecy when distribution incurs no opportunity cost.[^1]

[^1]: I particularly despise the transaction costs of socially permissioning capital, such as, for instance, asking my parents to acquire online materials I found beneficial. To bypass this gate, I invested significant energies into a cookie-dough fundraising campaign, selling something to the order of 85 packets of sweets door-to-door over the course of weeks. As the highest distributor at my school, I received a greatly cherished $75 Visa gift card, which I was ecstatic to use for anything I could want with fewer funding intermediaries (as a bearer-ish instrument).

[^2]: I intend to make the remainder public in redacted form in due time. I just haven't gotten around to censoring out the PII in the documents. That sensitive information is the chief (dare I say only) reason the remaining response letters are not public.

## Writing Content

Over the years transitioning from proprietary to open-source policy drafting, I've discovered my own lines based on extensive monitoring of comment and response performance.[^3] This mostly relates to how I've made my draft "sticky notes" thoughts private, as discussed in the last post. I use this raw working archive to jot down rudimentary ideas and inspirational sources for comment narratives and notes.

From the first comment, I've had a very clear idea of where I wanted my work to go. It's not that I know every path this DUNA journey would take, but I did have a good concept of the endpoint I wanted for the idea I formed at 16. That's helped me form a chain of comments[^4] politicking the Commission to a certain point of authority and credibility.

I don't think every letter we write needs to fit into some huge overarching plan or regulatory concept. But it is how I approach the writing, which is somewhat an inspiration for taking those brainstorming thoughts private, so I can speculate on ideas without committing to what was previously a public URL or flow that could be interpreted as a position even when half-formed. All this to say that I do appreciate WhatCan's perspective that private drafting allows for more flexibility and decisiveness, among other benefits.[^5]

[^3]: This has included hawk-eyeing all public Commission and other bureaucracy statements around and after the submission of my comments, the latter generally defined by the Regulatory Flexibility Act's timelines. Some analysis is relatively simple (checking how my comments are referenced in the final rule), while others take a little more nuance (examining Commissioner speeches on the basis of implied comprehension of my work). And I've received clear, explicit feedback in the form of rule adoptions or rejections alongside examination findings which mimic or deviate from oral finding chats.

[^4]: Not unironically inspired by blockchain.

[^5]: It took me a long time to realize that, having championed transparency for so long. And I still do! But there is a reasonable place for limited individual thoughts which, on their own, do not affect the whole Association or imply substantive private collaboration on public goods outside of the openness of our GitHub or forums.

## Substance Choice

I want the best possible (American) stock market above anybody and any other interests such as profits. This set of goals and values has led me to see open drafting as the best, if not the only, way to create regulatory submissions. The challenge, which I admit does not apply to most community members, is that secret internal (offline) drafting can insert predatory interests with no public record for future reference. When all the communication, intent, and original stance contents live on branch commits, others can call you out when you step away from the mission.[^6]

Consider a policy recommendation to increase the minimum account value needed to initiate a lost investor search, which hasn't been updated for inflation in decades. Such a comment would need to propose a dollar amount for the new minimum position value, in my opinion. While the whole comment will spell out the justification for this change, getting to that actual single number is a more comprehensive discussion amidst community stakeholders and their values.

It is challenging to fully capture a forum-style discussion in a single PDF with a cohesive message. As an alternative to a single statement, I greatly advocate for all community members to put in their own comments stating what they think is right on an issue like this (and why).[^7] However, let's consider the strict case of organizing a more comprehensive Association letter like the comment letter templates we've seen well drafted in the past.

I don't think it's professional or credible to repeat verbatim all the debate around a final output within a comment, cluttering it up with an appendix of message histories that take a lot of regulatory time and bandwidth to parse. I would much prefer we can all sit down and talk the item out together in a clear and public place until a consensus forms around a plausible position.[^8] GitHub works extremely well as a medium for such discussions and final collaborative output.

[^6]: This happened to me in the Stellar documentation work a couple years ago. I started a branch guiding open-source contributions using a draft PR. The repository maintainers disagreed with the political subterfuge I admittedly started traversing in my drafting remarks, and they called me out to fix it in public comments on the PR.

[^7]: It's really not that tough; the Commission makes web forms available and simple for all their rulemaking.

[^8]: For contentious items, this may even lead to a DUNA vote on position stances. I could well imagine something like this on a CAT-style rule which affects standardized interfaces between transfer agents. And, bonus, such a vote and its associated discussions make for great footnote references.

### Want for Decentralization

I believe the best opinions and output come from an informed crowd. It's why I think a DUNA-owned and operated TA standard will continually best any central private schema. There's just no CEO who can better forecast the future for their team compared to an idealized market-based organizational structure where every participant, from working member to end consumer, votes on direction.[^9] At least in the case of boring, predictable public infrastructure with clear roles and expectations in society.

[^9]: This isn't entirely true for new products or true trailblazing innovators. I don't think we get general public AI without someone like Altman persevering in the early years as a champion bringing something relatively completely new to the world. The world needs the craziness of those like Jobs, even if they may not be the best long-term fit for operations.
